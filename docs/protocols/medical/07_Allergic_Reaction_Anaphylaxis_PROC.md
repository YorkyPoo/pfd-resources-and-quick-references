# Poquoson Fire and Rescue Patient Care Protocols
## Medical - Allergic Reaction / Anaphylaxis

**OVERVIEW:**
This protocol outlines the management of patients experiencing an acute allergic reaction or severe anaphylaxis. A mild-to-moderate allergic reaction typically involves localized symptoms, while anaphylaxis is a severe, systemic, and potentially life-threatening event involving rapid onset of mucosal/skin, respiratory, cardiovascular, or gastrointestinal compromise.

### Assessment & Criteria
=== "History of Present Illness"
    - Exposure to a known or likely allergen (e.g., insect sting, food, medication)
    - Rapid onset of symptoms following exposure

=== "Signs & Symptoms"
    - **Mild/Moderate:** Localized signs or symptoms of an acute allergic reaction
    - **Anaphylaxis (Severe):** Involvement of skin or mucosal tissue
    - Respiratory distress
    - Hypotension or syncope
    - Persistent gastrointestinal symptoms (abdominal cramps, diarrhea, vomiting)

=== "Differential Considerations"
    - Two or more severe symptoms rapidly after exposure to a likely allergen indicates anaphylaxis
    - Hypotension alone after exposure to a known allergen indicates anaphylaxis

### Pearls
1. ⚠ EMTs must use either dose-specific clearly marked or color-coded syringes for **[[Epinephrine]]** IM.
2. ⚠ **[[Epinephrine]]** is the definitive treatment for anaphylaxis and should be administered immediately. Delaying **[[Epinephrine]]** administration can lead to poor outcomes.
3. Anaphylaxis requires two or more rapid onset symptoms (skin/mucosal, respiratory distress, hypotension, GI symptoms) after exposure to a likely allergen, or hypotension alone after exposure to a known allergen.
