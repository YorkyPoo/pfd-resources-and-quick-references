# Poquoson Fire and Rescue Patient Care Protocols
## Respiratory - Pneumothorax / Tension Pneumothorax

**OVERVIEW:**
Tension pneumothorax is a life-threatening condition where air accumulates in the pleural space under pressure, causing mediastinal shift, cardiovascular collapse, and respiratory failure. Immediate needle decompression is required to convert the tension pneumothorax to a simple pneumothorax, relieving the life-threatening pressure and restoring hemodynamic stability.

### Assessment & Criteria
=== "History of Present Illness"
    - Traumatic or spontaneous mechanism
    - Penetrating or blunt chest trauma
    - Recent invasive procedure (central line, thoracentesis)
    - COPD or asthma exacerbation
    - Mechanical ventilation

=== "Signs & Symptoms"
    - Respiratory distress
    - Hemodynamic instability (hypotension, tachycardia)
    - Absent or diminished breath sounds on affected side
    - Tracheal deviation (late sign)
    - Jugular venous distension (JVD)
    - Tachypnea, hypoxia
    - Cyanosis
    - Traumatic cardiac arrest with signs/suspicion of thoracoabdominal trauma

=== "Differential Considerations"
    - **Tension Pneumothorax**
    - Simple Pneumothorax
    - Hemothorax
    - Pulmonary Contusion
    - Cardiac Tamponade
    - Flail Chest
    - Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS)
    - Airway Obstruction

### Pearls
1. **⚠ CPAP is contraindicated in patients with pneumothorax.** Positive pressure ventilation may worsen the condition or cause tension physiology.
2. **⚠ Positive pressure ventilation (BVM/CPAP) may worsen or induce pneumothorax.** Use caution when ventilating patients with suspected pneumothorax.
3. **⚠ Bilateral Chest Decompression in Traumatic Cardiac Arrest:** For traumatic cardiac arrest with signs/suspicion of thoracoabdominal trauma, perform bilateral chest decompression to address potential bilateral tension pneumothorax.
4. **Equipment Selection:** Use the longest available needle (**3.25 inch** preferred) for adequate depth to reach the pleural space, especially in obese or muscular patients. Standard 1.5-inch needles may be insufficient.
5. **⚠ Needle Advancement Safety:** Once the "pop" is felt or air/blood/fluid is freely aspirated, **do NOT advance the needle further** to avoid injury to underlying structures (lung, heart, great vessels).
6. **Anatomical Landmarks:** Primary site is 2nd intercostal space at mid-clavicular line. Alternate acceptable position is 4th intercostal space at mid-axillary line (preferred in some obese patients or when mid-clavicular approach is suboptimal).
7. **Reassessment for Recurrence:** Tension pneumothorax can recur even after successful decompression. Continuously reassess vital signs, respiratory effort, and compliance. If deterioration occurs, repeat the procedure immediately.
8. **⚠ Breath Stacking in Obstructive Airway Disease:** If breath stacking occurs in patients with obstructive airway disease, disconnect the BVM and allow the patient to exhale.
9. **Hemodynamic Support:** No specific medications treat pneumothorax itself. Support is directed at correcting hypovolemia/shock with fluids and managing associated conditions.
10. **Catheter Patency:** The catheter may become occluded with blood or tissue. Monitor for reaccumulation of tension and be prepared to place additional catheters if needed.
