# Poquoson Fire and Rescue Patient Care Protocols
## Respiratory - Upper Airway Obstruction / Foreign Body Airway Obstruction

### Overview
Management of acute or impending airway obstruction including expanding hematoma, edema, mass, or foreign body obstruction. Requires rapid recognition and appropriate intervention based on patient consciousness, age, and body habitus. Foreign body airway obstruction (FBAO) is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate mechanical relief.

### Assessment & Criteria
=== "History of Present Illness"
    - Acute or impending airway obstruction
    - Expanding hematoma, edema, or mass
    - Witnessed choking episode
    - Sudden onset during eating
    - History of trauma to neck/airway

=== "Signs & Symptoms"
    - Inability to maintain patent airway
    - Secretions, blood, vomit obstructing airway
    - Complete or partial obstruction
    - Stridor, hoarseness, or voice changes
    - Cyanosis or profound respiratory distress
    - Inability to speak, cough, or breathe

=== "Differential Considerations"
    - Foreign body aspiration
    - Anaphylaxis with angioedema
    - Traumatic airway compromise
    - Infection (epiglottitis, abscess)
    - Tumor or mass obstruction
    - Post-procedural airway edema

### Pearls
1. **Conscious vs. Unconscious Algorithm:**<br>Conscious patients receive mechanical relief (Heimlich/chest thrusts). Unconscious patients receive airway positioning, suctioning, BLS adjuncts, and advanced airway interventions as indicated.

2. **⚠ Pediatric <1 Year Critical Positioning:**<br>Infants MUST be positioned with head DOWN, below the level of the chest during back blows and chest thrusts. Gravity assists in foreign body expulsion. Never perform abdominal thrusts on infants <1 year.

3. **Obesity and Pregnancy Modifications:**<br>⚠ Use CHEST thrusts (not abdominal) for pregnant women at any gestational age and markedly obese adults where abdominal thrusts cannot encircle the abdomen. Position fist midline against sternum, not abdomen.

4. **⚠ Paramedic Red Dot Skill - Pediatric Intubation:**<br>Endotracheal intubation of children <12 years of age is restricted to Paramedic "red dot" skill only. Intermediate providers must contact Medical Control or use SGA for pediatric patients.

5. **No Blind Finger Sweeps:**<br>⚠ Never perform blind finger sweeps in any age group. This may push the foreign body deeper into the airway, converting a partial obstruction to a complete obstruction. Use direct visualization (laryngoscopy + forceps) only.

6. **Complete vs. Partial Obstruction:**<br>Complete obstruction = unable to speak, cough, or breathe (silent, cyanotic). Requires immediate intervention. Partial obstruction = patient can cough forcefully or make sounds. Encourage coughing; do NOT interfere unless condition worsens.

7. **Post-Obstruction Care:**<br>All patients with foreign body removal require transport for evaluation due to risk of airway edema, aspiration pneumonitis, or retained fragments. Monitor closely for delayed complications even if obstruction appears relieved.

8. **Expanding Hematoma / Angioedema:**<br>⚠ Patients with rapidly expanding neck hematoma or angioedema (anaphylaxis, ACE inhibitor reaction) may decompensate without warning. Early aggressive airway management BEFORE complete obstruction is critical. Consider standing at head of bed, difficult airway preparation, and early Paramedic intercept.
