# Poquoson Fire and Rescue Patient Care Protocols
## General - Crush Syndrome & Entrapment

Guidelines for managing prolonged entrapment >30 minutes and crush injuries, focusing on the prevention and treatment of hyperkalemia before and after extrication.

### Assessment & Criteria
=== "History of Present Illness"
    - Any patient with a trapped/crushed extremity or torso for more than 30 minutes.
    - Prolonged suspension in a harness or prolonged time laying on a hard surface.

=== "Signs & Symptoms"
    - **ECG changes suggestive of hyperkalemia:**
    - Peaked T waves
    - Prolonged PR segment (>200 ms)
    - Flattening or loss of P waves
    - Wide QRS complex (Adult > 120 ms, Pediatric > 90 ms)
    - Ectopic beats
    - Arrhythmia (e.g., ventricular tachycardia)
    - Cardiac arrest

=== "Differential Considerations"
    - Risk of hemorrhage vs. fluid resuscitation.
    - Crush syndrome releases lethal amounts of potassium from ischemic muscle.

### Pearls
• ⚠ **Administer [[Sodium Bicarbonate]] and [[Calcium Chloride]] in separate lines to prevent precipitation** into Calcium Carbonate. If separate IV sites cannot be obtained, flush with at least **20 mL** saline between drugs.
• ⚠ **IV fluids may worsen bleeding.** Use clinical judgement when administering fluids to patients with crush injuries, balancing risk of hemorrhage vs benefit in treating crush syndrome.
• ⚠ **Crush syndrome** involves the release of toxins, particularly large amounts of potassium, from skeletal muscle cells following blunt trauma and prolonged soft tissue ischemia. Prehospital treatment is focused on preventing or treating life-threatening arrhythmia due to hyperkalemia.
• Crush injuries are not limited to heavy weights applied to extremities for a prolonged time and can occur in a variety of situations such as after prolonged suspension in a harness or following a prolonged time laying on a hard surface.
• Continuous **[[Albuterol]]** is given to treat suspected hyperkalemia in the setting of persistent ECG changes.
• Widened QRS: Adult > 120 ms, Pediatric > 90 ms.
