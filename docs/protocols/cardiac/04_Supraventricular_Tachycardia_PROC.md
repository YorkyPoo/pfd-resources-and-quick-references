# Poquoson Fire and Rescue Patient Care Protocols
## Cardiac - Supraventricular Tachycardia

### Overview
Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT) is a rapid rhythm originating above the ventricles, characterized by a narrow QRS complex and rate typically exceeding 150 beats per minute. Management is stratified based on patient stability, with stable patients receiving vagal maneuvers and pharmacologic intervention while unstable patients require immediate synchronized cardioversion to restore perfusion.

### Assessment & Criteria
=== "History of Present Illness"
    - Sudden onset palpitations
    - History of prior tachyarrhythmias
    - Medication history (including anticoagulants)
    - Congestive heart failure history
    - Recent illness or dehydration

=== "Signs & Symptoms"
    - Narrow complex tachycardia (Adult < 120 ms, Peds < 90 ms)
    - Rate usually > 150 bpm
    - Absent or abnormal P waves
    - Chest pain
    - Dyspnea
    - Altered mental status
    - Pulmonary edema
    - Signs of shock

=== "Differential Considerations"
    - Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response
    - Atrial flutter
    - Ventricular tachycardia (for wide complex)
    - Sinus tachycardia
    - Accessory pathway mediated tachycardia (WPW)
    - Multifocal atrial tachycardia
    - Concurrent CHF or decreased ejection fraction

### Pearls
1. Many patients who experience tachyarrhythmias have concurrent congestive heart failure or decreased ejection fraction. Exercise caution when providing IV fluids – give only enough to improve perfusion and monitor for signs of pulmonary edema.

2. In rare cases, electrical cardioversion to sinus rhythm can result in embolization of small blood clots from the heart, leading to stroke. Following emergent cardioversion, monitor closely for changes in mental status or physical exam and reassess destination choice as appropriate.

3. Dosing of electrical therapy described above serves as a guideline only. Confirm agency dosing for cardioversion and defibrillation with device specific manufacturers recommendations.

4. ⚠ **Ensure the device is set to SYNC prior to any attempted cardioversion of a patient with a palpable pulse. Unsynchronized cardioversion/defibrillation may lead to ventricular fibrillation and cardiac arrest.**

5. If the rhythm is polymorphic ventricular tachycardia (Torsades de Pointes), synchronization may not be possible. This is the only time a patient with a palpable pulse is treated with unsynchronized defibrillation.
