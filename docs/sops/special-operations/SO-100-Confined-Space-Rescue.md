CONFINED SPACE RESCUE

I. PURPOSE

To provide guidelines during entry and
rescue operations in a confined space.

This SOP is not all-inclusive and cannot
encompass all situations that may be
encountered.

Il. APPLICABILITY

All career and volunteer personnel

III. SCOPE

This policy is designed to establish
guidelines for conducting confined space
rescue operations. Confined spaces
include caverns, tunnels, pipes, tanks, and
any other locations where ventilation and
access are restricted by the configuration
of the space. These factors may also apply
to basements or attics. Confined space
incidents may involve injured persons,
persons asphyxiated or overcome by toxic
substances, cave-ins or fires occurring

dealing with these situations.

IV. DEFINITION

A confined space is “any space which is not intended for continual
occupancy, has limited means of egress,” and has the potential for
physical, chemical, or atmospheric engulfment.

V. PROCEDURES

PHASE I: ARRIVE ON SCENE. TAKE COMMAND. SIZE UP.
The Poquoson Fire/Rescue Department shall assume command
and control of any incident involving confined space entry and
rescue within the City of Poquoson. They shall follow the
guidelines set by OSHA’s Confined Space 1910.146 and NFPA CS
1670 — 1999.

the accident to determine exactly what happened.

should be done.

e Ifno witness is present, Command may have to look for clues
on the scene that may indicate what has happened.

e Anassessment of the victim(s) should be done.

e Command should determine how many victims have been
affected.

PHASE III: ATMOSPHERIC MONITORING

e Atmospheric monitoring shall occur prior to and during all
entries into a confined space. It should be stressed that the lack
of positive or alarm level readings does not eliminate the
requirement for proper respiratory protection!

e Atmospheric monitoring should be accomplished at high and
low areas of the space.

e All atmospheres shall be tested for:

o Oxygen deficiency
o Oxygen excess

o Toxicity

o Flammability

e The following levels shall be considered as immediately
dangerous to life and health (DLH) environments:

o Oxygen Deficient < 19.5%

o Oxygen Enriched > 23.0%

o Flammability at 10% of Lower Flammable Limit (LFL).

o Toxicity shall be any limit whose numerical value exceeds
the Permissible Exposure Limit (PEL).

e Atmospheric monitoring shall occur during occupancy at
intervals dependent on the possibility of changing conditions,
but in no case less than hourly.

e Inthe event that, in the opinion of the incident commander
designee, the atmospheric readings become unsafe to continue
operations, all entry teams shall be removed from the space
immediately until such time as the atmospheric conditions are
corrected.

PHASE IV: ENTRY

e Once the best method and location for entry has been
determined, teams shall begin entry and reconnaissance /
rescue / recovery operations in the space.

e Entry decisions shall be made based on known locations of the
victims, safety of the opening, atmospheric readings, and ease
of recovery points.

e If possible, attempt a two-prong attack to reach the victim (s) if
their location is known or suspected.

e Prior to entry, each team member shall have his or her time of
entry logged. This function shall be assigned to one technician
who shall keep the IC or Operations Officer apprised of the
status of each team.

e Teams shall be limited to thirty (30) minutes in any space.

e Each team shall be assigned to rehab upon removal from the
space until re-hydrated and vital signs are within normal limits.

e Once inside the space:

o Assure adequate interior team communications.

o Assure adequate communications with the operations
exterior.

o Mark if necessary with chalk or other method entry and
movement patterns to assure egress.

o Move towards the suspected victim location as a team.

o Beware of elevation differences and unstable footing.

PHASE V: VICTIM REMOVAL
e Once the victim is set for removal, assure the following:

o Assure as much c-spine control as is possible based on the
space and the victim’s condition.

o Use removal systems on the exterior, which are applicable
to the size and weight of the victim.

o Mechanical advantage systems are much preferred over
manual hauling.

o Donot use electric winches, etc., to remove victims; these
allow little control and could result in dismemberment or
additional injury.

o Decide if the victim is to be removed head first or feet
first.

o Avoid the use of wristlets on patients with burns to the
extremities.

e Once the victim is clear from the space, remove all entry team
personnel and equipment.

PHASE VI: SAFETY CONSIDERATIONS
e If rigging, hauling, or use of rope hardware is needed in the

space, assure only aluminum carabineers and hardware are
used to avoid sparks.
