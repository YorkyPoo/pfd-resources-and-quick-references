    Poquoson Fire and Rescue Patient Care Protocols

    Respiratory - Smoke Inhalation

    OVERVIEW: The management of smoke inhalation involves the assessment and treatment of airway injury from thermal and chemical exposure to combustion products, with particular attention to potential cyanide toxicity from fires in enclosed spaces. Patients exposed to smoke may develop progressive airway edema requiring prompt intervention, and those from enclosed space fires are at high risk for cyanide poisoning necessitating specific antidote therapy.

    Assessment & Criteria

    • Fire/explosion in enclosed space
    • Duration of exposure
    • Type of materials burning (synthetics vs. natural)
    • Loss of consciousness at scene
    • Exposure to combustion products

    Airway Injury Indicators: - Singed facial or nasal hairs - Burns or soot on face, nose, or mouth - Hoarse voice or stridor - Carbonaceous sputum - Difficulty breathing/respiratory distress - Hypoxia, Tachypnea Cyanide Poisoning: - Altered mental status - Seizures - Hypotension - Cardiac arrest

    • Direct thermal injury to airway
    • Chemical irritation (acid gases, aldehydes)
    • Carbon monoxide toxicity
    • Cyanide toxicity (from combustion of synthetics)
    • Upper airway obstruction secondary to edema
    • Pneumonitis/ARDS development
    • Consider concomitant burn injuries

    Interventions

    # Action / Intervention EMR EMT A I P
    1 Universal Patient Care / Initial Patient Contact:
    • Follow Poquoson Protocols for Universal Patient Care
    • Remove patient from source and ensure scene safety
    • Initiate PPE appropriate for smoke/hazardous environment    		 • •
    2 Scene Safety and Scene Time Considerations:
    • Assess scene for ongoing hazard exposure
    • Limit scene time; prepare for rapid transport
    • Coordinate with fire department/hazmat as needed    		 • •
    3 Airway Assessment:
    • Inspect for singed facial/nasal hairs
    • Examine face, nose, and mouth for burns or soot
    • Auscultate for stridor, hoarseness, or voice changes
    • Assess sputum for carbonaceous material
    • Monitor respiratory effort and work of breathing    		 • •
    4 Initial Airway Management:
    • Position patient to optimize ventilation
    • Suction oropharynx as needed for soot/secretions
    • Apply high-flow oxygen via appropriate delivery device    		 • •
    5 Advanced Airway Considerations:
    • Consider endotracheal intubation versus immediate transport to the nearest emergency department if any signs or symptoms of airway injury develop
    • Follow Endotracheal Intubation procedure if proceeding
    • Continuously reassess airway patency during transport    		 •
    6 Endotracheal Intubation (If Indicated):
    • Perform endotracheal intubation per procedure
    ⚠ Intubation of spontaneously breathing patients without RSI is generally not recommended
    ⚠ Intubation of children under 12 years old is a Paramedic "red dot" skill only    		 MC MC
    7 Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanide Antidote) - Indication Assessment:
    • Assess for exposure to combustion products/fire in an enclosed space with ANY of the following:
    - Altered mental status
    - Seizures
    - Hypotension
    - Cardiac arrest    		 MC MC
    8 Hydroxocobalamin Preparation:
    • Reconstitute with 200 mL 0.9% Sodium Chloride
    • Use vented IV tubing for administration
    • Verify solution clarity and integrity prior to administration
    9 Hydroxocobalamin Administration:
    • Administer per Poquoson OMD guidance
    • Monitor for increase in blood pressure
    • Inform receiving facility of administration
    • Prepare for possible red/purple urine discoloration in patient    		 MC MC
    10 Burn Assessment:
    • Assess for concomitant thermal burn injuries
    • Estimate total body surface area (TBSA) involved
    • Evaluate burn depth and critical areas (face, hands, perineum, major joints)    		 • •
    11 Fluid Management (If Burns Present):
    • Initiate fluid resuscitation per Burn Injuries Protocol
    • Titrate fluid administration to hemodynamic response
    • Monitor urine output if catheter in place    		 •
    12 Medical Control Contact:
    • Contact Poquoson OMD for cyanide poisoning confirmation and specific Hydroxocobalamin dosing guidance
    • Contact Regional Burn Center for significant burn injuries requiring specialized care
    • Coordinate destination for combined airway/burn injuries    		 MC MC
    13 Monitoring and Reassessment:
    • Monitor SpO2, EtCO2, and respiratory rate continuously
    • Reassess airway patency frequently; edema may progress rapidly
    • Observe for signs of respiratory failure requiring immediate airway intervention    		 • •
    14 Transport Considerations:
    • Expedited transport given risk of rapid airway compromise
    • Transport to facility capable of managing combined trauma/burns if indicated
    • Consider aeromedical evacuation for significant burn centers if prolonged ground transport    		 • •

    Pearls

    1. Airway Injury Progression: Airway edema from smoke inhalation can progress rapidly over minutes to hours following exposure. Frequent reassessment and a low threshold for advanced airway intervention is essential in patients with any indicators of airway injury.

    2. ⚠ RSI Consideration: Intubation of spontaneously breathing patients without rapid sequence induction (RSI) is generally not recommended. Consider allowing patient to maintain spontaneous ventilation when possible, proceeding to intubation only if respiratory failure develops.

    3. ⚠ Pediatric Intubation: Endotracheal intubation of children under 12 years old is a strictly Paramedic "red dot" skill only. Intermediate providers MUST NOT intubate pediatric patients under 12 years of age.

    4. Hydroxocobalamin Mechanism: Hydroxocobalamin binds cyanide ions to form cyanocobalamin, which is excreted in the urine. This action effectively removes cyanide from cellular cytochrome oxidase, allowing aerobic metabolism to resume.

    5. ⚠ Hydroxocobalamin Side Effects: Administration of Hydroxocobalamin may cause significant increase in blood pressure; use caution in patients with severe hypertension. The medication causes deep red/purple discoloration of urine, skin, and mucous membranes which is expected and may persist for days.

    6. Preparation Critical: Hydroxocobalamin requires reconstitution with 200 mL 0.9% Sodium Chloride and MUST be administered using vented IV tubing. Failure to use proper preparation may result in ineffective delivery.

    7. Burn Center Referral: Contact the Regional Burn Center early for patients with significant burn injuries, especially those involving the airway or extensive total body surface area. Early coordination facilitates optimal definitive care.

    8. Multi-Toxicity Consideration: Patients from enclosed space fires may have combined carbon monoxide, cyanide, and thermal inhalation injuries. High-flow oxygen addresses carbon monoxide; Hydroxocobalamin addresses cyanide. Neither antidote negates the need for airway protection from thermal injury.
